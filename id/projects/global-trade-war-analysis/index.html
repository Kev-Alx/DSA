def calculate_caar(df_stocks, index_name, event_dates, est_window=90, evt_window=5): """ Calculates Cumulative Average Abnormal Returns (CAAR) for a given index. Uses a Mean-Adjusted Return model based on the estimation window. """ df_idx = df_stocks[df_stocks['index_name'] == index_name].copy() df_idx = df_idx.set_index('date').sort_index() df_idx['daily_return_pct'] = df_idx['daily_return_pct'].fillna(0) event_cars = [] for event_date in event_dates: event_date = pd.to_datetime(event_date) # Safely get integer locations for modern Pandas versions try: event_loc = df_idx.index.get_indexer([event_date], method='nearest')[0] if event_loc == -1: continue except KeyError: continue est_start = max(0, event_loc - evt_window - est_window) est_end = max(0, event_loc - evt_window) evt_start = max(0, event_loc - evt_window) evt_end = min(len(df_idx) - 1, event_loc + evt_window) # Estimation window to calculate baseline expected return estimation_data = df_idx.iloc[est_start:est_end] if len(estimation_data) < (est_window // 2): continue expected_return = estimation_data['daily_return_pct'].mean() # Event window calculations event_data = df_idx.iloc[evt_start:evt_end + 1].copy() if len(event_data) != (2 * evt_window + 1): continue event_data['abnormal_return'] = event_data['daily_return_pct'] - expected_return event_data['CAR'] = event_data['abnormal_return'].cumsum() event_data['event_day'] = range(-evt_window, evt_window + 1) event_cars.append(event_data.set_index('event_day')['CAR']) if not event_cars: return None # Aggregate to CAAR car_df = pd.concat(event_cars, axis=1) caar = pd.DataFrame({ 'CAAR': car_df.mean(axis=1), 'STD': car_df.std(axis=1), 'N': car_df.count(axis=1) }) # 95% Confidence Interval t_crit = 1.96 caar['CI_upper'] = caar['CAAR'] + (t_crit * (caar['STD'] / np.sqrt(caar['N']))) caar['CI_lower'] = caar['CAAR'] - (t_crit * (caar['STD'] / np.sqrt(caar['N']))) return caar # Execute CAAR calculation for S&P 500 caar_sp500 = calculate_caar(df_stocks, 'S&P 500 (USA)', event_dates, est_window=90, evt_window=5) # Plotting the Event Study if caar_sp500 is not None: fig, ax = plt.subplots() ax.plot(caar_sp500.index, caar_sp500['CAAR'], color='#002E5D', lw=2.5, label='CAAR') ax.fill_between(caar_sp500.index, caar_sp500['CI_lower'], caar_sp500['CI_upper'], color='#6A737B', alpha=0.3, label='95% Confidence Interval') ax.axvline(0, color='#A2142F', linestyle='--', lw=2, label='Tariff Announcement (t=0)') ax.axhline(0, color='black', lw=1) ax.set_title("S&P 500 Cumulative Average Abnormal Returns (CAAR)\nAround Major Tariff Announcements") ax.set_xlabel("Trading Days Relative to Announcement") ax.set_ylabel("Cumulative Abnormal Return (%)") ax.legend(loc='upper left') plt.tight_layout() plt.show()

To evaluate the immediate equity market pricing of tariff implementations, a Cumulative Average Abnormal Return (CAAR) methodology is utilized. CAAR aggregates the daily impact of a specific event on asset prices by extracting expected benchmark returns, thereby isolating the abnormal pricing reaction directly attributable to the catalyst.

The wide 95% confidence band completely encompassing the zero line throughout the entire event window from the chart indicates that major tariff announcements do not produce a statistically significant directional shock to the aggregate S&P 500.

Key Findings:

  1. Markets don’t really react sharply to tariff announcements at the index level. Big indices like the S&P 500 tend to absorb or already price in the news, so you don’t see a clear jump or drop in the few days around the event.
  2. What does change is the level of uncertainty. After tariffs are announced, market movements become more spread out, suggesting increased volatility as money shifts between sectors that are hurt and those that benefit.

What is GARCH?

To quantify the transmission of trade policy uncertainty into foreign exchange markets, a Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity (GARCH) model is applied to the USD/CNY exchange rate returns. GARCH models time-varying volatility, specifically capturing the phenomenon of "variance clustering"—where large price fluctuations are followed by large fluctuations, and small by small. By estimating conditional variance, this methodology identifies how long a shock to the currency market persists before reverting to the mean.

Analysis

The model yields a highly significant β\betaβ coefficient of 0.8929 and an α\alphaα coefficient of 0.0927. The sum of these parameters (α+β≈0.985\alpha + \beta \approx 0.985α+β≈0.985) approaches unity, indicating extreme volatility persistence. A tariff-induced shock to the USD/CNY exchange rate does not dissipate quickly; rather, it fundamentally alters the risk regime for an extended duration. This is visually confirmed by the sustained volatility clusters aligning directly with major tariff announcements across the historical timeline.

Furthermore, the post-tariff depreciation matrix and the primary exchange rate trajectory reveal a distinct structural break in currency market behavior. During the initial phases of the trade war (2018–2024), targeted tariffs consistently triggered CNY depreciation (ranging from 0.04% to 2.95% over 30 days), acting as a natural pricing shock absorber for exporters. However, the implementation of broad "All Goods" tariffs beginning in early 2025 marks a definitive regime shift, paradoxically resulting in immediate CNY appreciation (-0.23% to -1.55%) following the announcements.

Section Summary

  1. High Volatility Persistence: GARCH(1,1) estimations confirm that trade-induced FX volatility is highly persistent. Sudden geopolitical escalations create prolonged periods of currency instability rather than transient, quickly resolved pricing anomalies.
  2. Currency as an Absorption Mechanism: Early, targeted tariffs (2018–2024) systematically precipitated predictable CNY depreciation, effectively neutralizing a portion of the nominal tariff impact and shifting the cost burden dynamically.
  3. Structural Regime Shift: The transition to sweeping "All Goods" tariffs in 2025 triggered a definitive break in foreign exchange mechanics. The resulting contrarian CNY appreciation signals a fundamental exhaustion of the currency's capacity to unilaterally absorb broad-based macroeconomic trade barriers.

Causal Impact Evaluation using Difference-in-Differences (DiD) Modeling

Difference-in-Differences (DiD) is a methodology utilized to isolate and estimate the causal effect of a specific intervention. In this context, the implementation of a tariff. The model compares the post-event trajectory of a "treatment" group (sectors highly exposed to tariffs) against a "control" group (domestically insulated sectors). By evaluating the differential in performance before and after the event, the DiD estimator strips out broader market movements that affect all sectors simultaneously, revealing the isolated impact of the policy.

The Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) estimation yields an interaction coefficient—the core DiD estimator—of 0.0341. However, with a p-value of 0.831, this result is highly statistically insignificant. This indicates that the initial tariff implementation did not exert a definitive, causal drag on the returns of targeted sectors relative to insulated sectors in the immediate aftermath.

13. Key Findings

  1. Trade wars caused significant but temporary market dislocations.
  • Event studies show no statistically significant directional shocks in the immediate trading window.
  • Major tariff announcements also do not produce statistically significant shocks in broad equity indices (e.g., S&P 500). Markets largely pre-price or absorb these events.
  • Extreme market stress is not caused by trade wars. Major anomalies align with systemic crises (e.g., 2020 pandemic), not tariff announcements.
  1. Volatility—not returns—is the primary transmission channel

  • Across equities and FX, the dominant impact is persistent volatility rather than directional price movement. Trade shocks reshape risk regimes instead of market levels.

  • Markets are primarily internally driven. ~59% of market dynamics are explained by volatility, indicating endogenous factors dominate over tariff events.

  1. FX acts as a dynamic shock absorber—until it doesn’t

  • 2018–2024 (Trade War 1.0 & Recovery): Tariffs → CNY depreciation (offsetting tariff costs)

  • 2025 onward (Trade War 2.0): Structural break → CNY appreciation

  • This indicates limits to currency adjustment mechanisms under prolonged trade pressure.

  1. Sectoral divergence is the real impact Trade-exposed sectors (e.g., semiconductors, autos) show higher returns and higher volatility, while domestic sectors remain more stable but with lower returns.

  2. No strong short-term causal damage to targeted sectors DiD results show no statistically significant immediate impact, suggesting rapid market adjustment.

  3. Trade wars rewire—not reduce—global trade Short-term decline in trade volume is followed by recovery via rerouting, with countries like Vietnam, Mexico, and India benefiting.

  4. Macroeconomic impact is asymmetric Trade volume is persistently negatively affected, while inflation effects are weak and inconsistent. Trade wars act more as growth shocks than inflation drivers.

  5. Tariffs behave as exogenous policy shocks VAR and FEVD results show tariffs strongly influence trade flows but are not driven by macroeconomic variables.

  6. Market impact is driven by tariff scope rather than intensity High-intensity retaliatory tariffs show limited or mixed market effects, while broad, system-wide tariffs (large trade coverage) generate the most negative returns and highest volatility, indicating that scale and systemic reach matter more than tariff magnitude alone.

14. Strategic Reccomendations

For Policymakers

  1. Anticipate trade diversion Protectionist policies reallocate trade rather than reduce it. Plan for third-country spillovers and supply chain shifts.

  2. Strengthen supply chain resilience Invest in domestic capacity and strategic partnerships (friend-shoring) to mitigate persistent trade volume shocks.

  3. Monitor financial stability through volatility Track FX volatility and sector-level dispersion as primary indicators of trade shock impact.

For Traders

  1. Trade volatility, not direction Tariff events are better suited for options strategies and volatility-based approaches rather than directional bets.

  2. Rotate selectively into trade-exposed sectors These sectors offer higher returns but with higher risk, requiring active management and hedging.

  3. Avoid overreacting to tariff headlines Aggregate market responses are muted, so avoid panic-driven decisions.

15. Risks and Limitations

  1. Data limitations Tariff data may not fully capture non-tariff barriers, and financial variables act as indirect proxies of economic impact.

  2. Model assumptions GARCH may miss nonlinear regime shifts, while VAR differencing may remove long-term relationships.

  3. Statistical insignificance in causal models DiD results may reflect either market efficiency or limited model sensitivity.

  4. Concurrent COVID-19 and monetary policy effects may confound results Overlapping shocks during the sample period may blur the isolated impact of trade war policies.

  5. Bilateral trade data needed for precise trade diversion measurement Aggregate trade data limits the ability to accurately capture rerouting effects across specific country pairs.
